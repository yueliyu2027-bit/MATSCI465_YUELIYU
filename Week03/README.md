In Task 1, we established a classical image-processing baseline pipeline for TEM image analysis. The workflow included image normalization and contrast enhancement, threshold-based segmentation (Otsu method), morphological refinement to remove small artifacts, and connected-component analysis to extract quantitative morphological descriptors such as area, perimeter, eccentricity, and coverage ratio. In addition, FFT-based validation was introduced as a physics-informed consistency check. Quantitatively, clustering evaluation showed the best performance at 𝑘=3 with a Silhouette score of 0.31, while higher k values led to decreased separation (0.261 for 𝑘=5, 0.239 for 𝑘=7). These results indicate limited feature separability and sensitivity of threshold-based segmentation to noise and low-contrast regions.

In Task 2, we extended the baseline by constructing a feature matrix from extracted morphological and intensity-based descriptors, followed by feature selection, train–test splitting with stratification, and supervised model training with standardized inputs. Compared with the unsupervised clustering in Task 1, the supervised framework improved classification robustness and reduced misclassification in complex backgrounds. Overall, Task 1 provides a simple and interpretable baseline suitable for clean images, while Task 2 demonstrates improved quantitative performance and scalability. Future improvements could include more discriminative feature engineering, better denoising strategies, and incorporation of physics-informed constraints directly into the learning framework to further enhance reliability and reproducibility.

In task3, a reproducible TEM image analysis pipeline was developed by integrating classical image processing, machine learning, and deep learning methods. All images were normalized and resized to 416×416 for consistency. Due to the absence of pixel-level annotations, pseudo masks were generated using intensity-based thresholding, which provides a physically reasonable approximation of density regions in TEM images and enables weakly supervised U-Net training.

For Task 3, a U-Net model with encoder–decoder architecture and skip connections was implemented, along with extended data augmentation (rotation, shift, zoom, flips, brightness variation, and noise) to improve generalization. Intermediate feature maps were visualized to enhance model interpretability and demonstrate hierarchical feature learning.

Quantitative and qualitative comparisons show that classical methods are fast and interpretable but sensitive to noise and contrast variations, while CNN performs well for global classification but lacks spatial precision. U-Net achieves the best segmentation consistency and spatial accuracy, especially in complex TEM backgrounds.

Overall, classical methods are suitable for quick baseline analysis, whereas U-Net is recommended for high-precision pixel-level segmentation in computational microscopy due to its robustness, scalability, and superior spatial feature representation.
